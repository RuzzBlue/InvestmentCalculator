Understand the numbers behind “APR”

Simple interest

Interest is earned only on your original principal. If you invest $1,000 at 10% simple interest for 3 years, you earn $100 each year → $300 total interest. Balance ends at $1,300.

Interest = Principal × Rate × Time

Compound interest

Interest is earned on principal plus previously earned interest. Same $1,000 at 10% compounded annually for 3 years:

  • Year 1: $1,000 → $1,100
  • Year 2: $1,100 → $1,210
  • Year 3: $1,210 → $1,331

You earned an extra $31 just because interest itself earned interest. Over decades, that gap becomes the main story of investing.

What platforms hide: many products advertise a shiny “APR” but never say whether rewards compound back into the earning balance, sit in Spot as cash, or only settle at maturity (simple interest for the lock period).

APR — Annual Percentage Rate

The nominal yearly rate without including the effect of compounding. Crypto earn products almost always quote APR.

APY — Annual Percentage Yield

The effective yearly return including compounding. Banks and high-yield savings often quote APY because it looks higher for the same underlying rate.

APY = (1 + APR ÷ n)n − 1    where n = compounds per year

Example: 5% APR compounded daily ≈ 5.13% APY. Monthly ≈ 5.12% APY. Annually = exactly 5% APY.

Rule of thumb: when comparing a crypto “5% APR” to a bank “5% APY”, the bank product is slightly better if both truly compound — because APY already baked in the compounding. Always convert to the same measure before comparing.

Frequency changes the curve

Same APR, different compounding schedules, produce different balances:

Moving from annual → daily matters, but less than people expect for moderate rates. The bigger lever is usually how long you stay invested and whether contributions continue.

Contributions compound too

Money you add each month also starts earning. A calculator that only compounds the starting amount understates real dollar-cost-averaging plans.

Flexible / Simple Earn (Binance-style)

Rewards usually split into two layers:

  • Regular (real-time) APR — applies to your full subscribed balance. On Binance Flexible, it can accrue as often as every minute and is added back into Earn, so it does compound.
  • Bonus / tiered APR — promo rate on only the first X tokens (e.g. first $2,000). Typically calculated daily, paid to Spot, and does not compound into the Earn balance.
Daily bonus ≈ min(balance, bonusCap) × BonusAPR ÷ 365

Anything above the bonus cap only earns the regular APR.

Fixed Term / Locked

You lock funds for 7–120 days (etc.) at a fixed APR. Exchanges such as Bybit typically calculate:

Yield = Principal × APR ÷ 365 × LockDays

Principal + yield return at maturity. No compounding during the lock unless you manually reinvest into a new term after maturity.

Staking

You lock (or soft-lock) assets to secure a network or a platform staking pool. Rewards are usually quoted as APR/APY and credited periodically (often daily). Modeling daily compounding of the staking APR is a reasonable average assumption when rewards auto-restake; if rewards sit idle in Spot, treat them closer to simple interest unless you restake manually.

Promo trap: “Up to 200% APR” almost always means a tiny tier or a short campaign window. Always check the bonus cap and the regular APR that applies afterward.

What “return” means for an ETF

Total shareholder return comes from:

  1. Price appreciation — the ETF share price rises with its holdings.
  2. Dividends / distributions — cash paid from underlying stock dividends (and sometimes capital gains).
  3. Minus the expense ratio — the fund’s annual fee, taken continuously from assets (~0.03% for VOO, much higher for active funds).

DRIP vs cash dividends

With dividend reinvestment (DRIP), distributions buy more shares and compound. Without DRIP, price grows net of expenses, while dividends accumulate as separate cash (they still count as earnings, but they stop compounding inside the ETF).

Net growth ≈ PriceReturn + (DividendYield if reinvested) − ExpenseRatio

Withdrawal fees

Some apps charge a fee when you cash out. That fee does not change how the investment grows — it only reduces what you actually receive when you leave. YieldLens shows both balances when a withdrawal fee is set.

How to read exchange and broker pages

  • Is the number labeled APR or APY?
  • Does interest go back into the earning product, or into Spot/Funding?
  • Is there a bonus tier cap (first $X only)?
  • Fixed term = usually simple interest for the lock.
  • For ETFs: check dividend yield, expense ratio, and whether your broker supports DRIP.
  • Rates change — projections are hypothetical, not forecasts.
